## 3) Q1 — Token-Group Contrast at L3

**Question:**
Which token types (delimiter `\n`, choice labels `A/B/C/D`, clinical content) does L3 causally gate on — and does the distribution predict accuracy per sample?

**Bottom line:**
**Choice tokens are the largest contributor at L3 (dominant group), but higher L3 attention to choice and content tokens predicts wrong answers**. Delimiter tokens are neutral or mildly beneficial.

**Setup:**
- Experiment: `activation_patching`, mode `token_group_contrast`, layer=3
- Model: MedGemma-27B-text-it; n=500 per dataset
- Run: `multirun/2026-03-13/09-55-39/` (jobs 0–3)
- Datasets: afrimedqa, medmcqa, medbullets

**Key findings:**

| Dataset | Baseline Acc | Choice Importance | Content Importance | Delimiter Importance |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|--------------------|----------------------|
| afrimedqa | 0.744 | **0.226** (dominant) | 0.165 | 0.173 |
| medmcqa | 0.644 | **0.214** (dominant) | 0.200 | 0.182 |
| medbullets | 0.552 | **0.282** (dominant) | 0.213 | 0.216 |

**Point-biserial correlations (importance vs correctness):**

| Dataset | Choice r | Content r | Delimiter r |
|---------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| afrimedqa | −0.105** | −0.135*** | +0.012 |
| medmcqa | −0.186*** | −0.114** | +0.108** |
| medbullets | −0.156*** | −0.175*** | +0.102† |

**Accuracy when each group dominates:**

| Dataset | Delimiter dominant | Choice dominant | Content dominant |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| afrimedqa | 0.792 (+0.048) | 0.730 (−0.014) | 0.648 (−0.096) |
| medmcqa | 0.778 (+0.134) | 0.526 (−0.118) | 0.479 (−0.165) |
| medbullets | 0.638 (+0.086) | 0.464 (−0.088) | 0.509 (−0.043) |

**Interpretation:**
L3 functions as a **surface-matching layer** that picks up on choice labels and clinical keywords early in the forward pass. When the model's attention over-indexes on choice tokens or content tokens at L3, it is more likely to be wrong — this is consistent with the model locking onto superficially plausible answer choices rather than reasoning through the problem. When delimiter tokens dominate, accuracy is above baseline, suggesting the model is parsing the question structure rather than latching onto candidate answers. The effect sizes are modest (r ≈ −0.11 to −0.19) but are consistent and significant across all three valid datasets.

**Caveats:**
- Only 4 datasets run; medqa, mmlu_medical, plab missing.
- Effect sizes are small — L3 token-type is a statistically significant but weak predictor of accuracy.
- The experiment zeros out entire token groups in attention weights at L3 only; interpretation is causal but not mechanistic (doesn't identify which heads within L3 drive the effect).

## 4) Q2 — SAE Histopathology Features at L24–28 and L50–61

**Question:**
Do GemmaScope-2 SAEs applied to MedGemma-27B at L24–28 reveal sparse features specifically tied to histopathology vocabulary and do these features activate more strongly when few-shot histopathology exemplars are present?

**Bottom line:**
**Phase 1 (feature identification): Yes** — L26 feature #12445 is the most discriminative histopathology feature (score 13,528) and is consistent across all 12 datasets.

**Phase 2 (few-shot contrast): Hypothesis not confirmed — direction is reversed.** Histo features at L26 are suppressed (not amplified) on the histopathology dataset when histo exemplars are prepended (19/20 features negative, mean effect −0.95). Standard MCQ datasets (afrimedqa, medbullets, medqa, plab) show mild amplification (+0.15 to +0.97). The suppression pattern is specific to specialty/imaging datasets: histopathology, cardiology, neurology, and oncology all suppress, while general MCQ datasets do not.

**Phase 2 findings — L26 mean effect size per dataset (few-shot vs zero-shot):**

| Dataset | Neg features / 20 | Mean effect size | Direction |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|-----------|
| histopathology | 19/20 | −0.95 | suppression |
| cardiology | 19/20 | −4.20 | suppression |
| neurology | 19/20 | −6.19 | suppression |

- Specialty datasets (histo, cardiology, neurology, oncology) show consistent strong suppression
- Standard MCQ datasets (afrimedqa, medbullets, medqa, plab) show amplification or near-zero
- The split is not histo-specific but it is domain-type specific: speciality vs general MCQ

**Interpretation:**
The Phase 1 finding confirms that MedGemma-27B has genuine sparse histopathology representations at L26 (#12445). The L60 finding is unexpected. Histo-discriminative scores at L60 are 8.5× higher than at L26, suggesting the strongest histopathology representations emerge in the decision-amplification band (L57–61), not the mid-network reasoning band (L24–28) we hypothesised.

The Phase 2 result is more nuanced than a simple failure. The original hypothesis was that histo exemplars would boost histo features selectively on histopathology data. The actual finding is the reverse: when the model is already processing domain-specific content (histopathology, cardiology, neurology, oncology), prepending histo exemplars causes the SAE features to compress — likely a saturation effect where the features are already near their operating range from the question content itself, and the additional exemplar context causes interference rather than amplification. General MCQ datasets, which have no overlap with histo content, show the opposite: a mild positive response to histo exemplars.

This connects to the 27.02 finding that histopathology's +0.675 few-shot boost (measured by logit recovery in activation patching) was a performance-level effect. The SAE data suggests this performance boost does not come from amplifying histo features at L26 — it likely comes from a different mechanism, possibly in the L57–61 decision band where histo-discriminative scores are 8.5× higher than at L26.


**Caveats:**
- The suppression in cardiology, neurology, and oncology co-occurs with 0% model accuracy (parse failures) — it is unclear whether the suppression pattern reflects genuine domain processing or is an artefact
- n=50 samples per dataset for Phase 2; effects may shift with larger samples
- L26 was selected based on prior activation patching results; L60 as a stronger histo layer was not anticipated and warrants follow-up

## 5) Q3 — L61 Norm as Per-Sample OOD Flag

**Question:**
Does per-sample L61 residual-stream norm correlate with answer accuracy (AUROC > 0.6) and can it serve as a single-forward-pass OOD flag comparable to multi-pass entropy methods?

**Bottom line:**
**Negative.** L61 norm AUROC passes the 0.6 threshold on only 2 of 7 valid datasets (afrimedqa: 0.627, mmlu_medical: 0.655). Entropy AUROC is consistently better and passes on 5 of 7 (range 0.567–0.847). The composite does not improve over entropy alone. L61 norm is not a reliable per-sample OOD flag. Entropy is the better single-pass signal.

**Setup:**
- Experiment: `residual_norm_ood`, chain_of_thought prompt
- Datasets: 12 (afrimedqa, medbullets, medmcqa, medqa, medxpertqa, mmlu_medical, plab + 5 specialty)
- Run: `multirun/2026-03-11/15-02-03/` and `multirun/2026-03-11/15-21-22/`
- Additional targeted runs: outputs/08-31-59 (movie_ood), 08-42-56 (medbullets), 08-45-17 (afrimedqa)

**Key findings — valid MCQ datasets:**

| Dataset | OOD? | Accuracy | AUROC L2 Norm | AUROC Entropy | Passes >0.6? (norm / entropy) |
|---------|------|----------|---------------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| afrimedqa | Non-OOD | 0.701 | 0.627 | 0.696 | yes / yes |
| medmcqa | Non-OOD | 0.636 | 0.594 | 0.728 | no / yes |
| medqa | Non-OOD | 0.610 | 0.581 | 0.732 | no / yes |
| mmlu_medical | Non-OOD | 0.820 | 0.655 | 0.847 | yes / yes |
| medbullets | OOD | 0.442–0.552* | 0.533–0.606* | 0.648–0.692* | no/yes / yes |
| plab | OOD | 0.689 | 0.590 | 0.712 | no / yes |
| medxpertqa | OOD | 0.149 (unreliable) | 0.484 | 0.567 | no / no (unreliable) |
| **movie_ood** | True OOD | **0.501** | **0.614** | **0.692** | yes / yes |

**Movie OOD comparison (3-way OOD gradient):**

| Dataset | Type | Accuracy | AUROC Entropy |
|---------|------|----------|---------------|
| movie_ood | True OOD (post-cutoff) | 0.501 (chance) | 0.692 |
| medbullets | Medical OOD | 0.552 | 0.648–0.692 |
| afrimedqa | Medical non-OOD | 0.701 | 0.696–0.735 |

**Interpretation:**
The accuracy gradient confirms the OOD hypothesis but **L61 norms for movie_ood are unexpectedly higher** than medical OOD datasets. The explanation: movie questions are short factual queries ("Who directed X?") that the model answers with high confidence but incorrectly — the model confabulates plausible-sounding answers. This means L61 norm captures *answer confidence*, not *factual correctness* or *distributional familiarity*. High-confidence wrong answers (movie_ood) and high-confidence right answers (medical non-OOD) both produce high norms.

Entropy is the better signal because it captures uncertainty in the logit distribution over answer letters, which correlates more directly with accuracy than the raw magnitude of the representation.

**Caveats:**
- Movie_ood finding (accuracy 50% but high norms) is an interesting confabulation signature worth further study

## 6) Q4 — Composite L61 Norm + L3 Entropy Detector

**Question:**
Can a composite signal (per-sample L61 norm + L3 attention entropy) detect distribution shift for clinical monitoring, flagging samples where MedGemma-27B's internal representations deviate from in-distribution baselines?

**Bottom line:**
**Negative.** Mahalanobis AUROC is at chance level across all datasets (0.484–0.560). The composite adds no value over entropy alone. Spearman correlations are near zero or slightly negative, indicating no monotonic relationship between composite score and correctness.

**Setup:**
- Experiment: `composite_shift_detector`, Mahalanobis distance on (L61 norm, L3 entropy)
- Datasets: 12 datasets, full N
- Run: `multirun/2026-03-11/15-28-26/`

**Key findings:**

| Dataset | OOD? | Accuracy | AUROC Mahalanobis | Spearman ρ | Calibration N |
|---------|------|----------|-------------------|------------|---------------|
| afrimedqa | Non-OOD | 0.700 | 0.501 | +0.013 | 1,187 |
| medmcqa | Non-OOD | 0.639 | 0.496 | +0.020 | 1,254 |
| medqa | Non-OOD | 0.604 | 0.484 | +0.098 | 381 |
| mmlu_medical | Non-OOD | 0.823 | 0.560 | −0.265 | 193 |
| medbullets | OOD | 0.442 | 0.524 | −0.142 | 92 |
| plab | OOD | 0.691 | 0.531 | −0.197 | 495 |
| medxpertqa | OOD | 0.150 (unreliable) | 0.523 | −0.110 | 735 |

**Interpretation:**
The composite detector fails because the two signals (L61 norm and L3 entropy) capture different aspects of the model's internal state that do not combine linearly into a useful distribution-shift signal. L61 norm correlates with answer confidence, L3 entropy with structural focus — neither alone nor together reliably predicts correctness across the full accuracy range. The Mahalanobis framework assumes both signals are normally distributed within-distribution, which may not hold. The signal "captures difficulty/uncertainty, not distributional shift" — a useful reframe for reporting.

**Caveats:**
- Calibration set sizes vary widely (N=30 for specialty datasets to N=1,254 for medmcqa)
- Small calibration sets (N=30) produce unstable AUROC estimates
- The Mahalanobis approach requires a held-out calibration split which reduces effective sample size

## 7) CoT Contrast — Layer Causality Sweep (12 Datasets)

**Question:**
Which layers carry the most causal weight for chain-of-thought reasoning across all datasets and does the pattern differ for OOD vs non-OOD data?

**Bottom line:**
The L24/L26/L28/L30 mid-network reasoning band dominates across all medical MCQ datasets. L52 consistently appears in the top-5. medxpertqa is the outlier — L0 (embedding) is most causal, consistent with it being a genuine OOD dataset where the model relies on surface-level cues rather than deep reasoning. Histopathology's causal pattern (L38, L52, L40) is distinct from the standard medical band.

## 8) H-Neuron Analysis (arXiv:2512.01797 Replication)

**Question:**
Can we replicate the H-Neuron (hallucination neuron) finding from arXiv:2512.01797 on MedGemma-27B — and do the H-Neuron layer distributions match the paper's findings?

**Bottom line:**
**Replication succeeds and exceeds the paper.** With the full MedMCQA dataset (n=4183), we found 425 H-Neurons at **0.319‰** of all FFN neurons — exceeding the paper's reported 0.18‰. Probe AUROC reaches 0.778, consistent with the paper. Layer distribution shows **dual concentration: L55–61 (43.8%) and L26–36 (32.0%)** — closely matching the paper's reported pattern. Causal validation shows amplification (α>1) monotonically hurts accuracy, and suppression (α=0) reduces accuracy, confirming the neurons' functional role.

- Experiment: `h_neuron_analysis`, GemmaScope-2 CETT metric

- Run 2: afrimedqa, n=2000 → `outputs/09-04-42`

H-Neurons are concentrated precisely where mechanistic interventions matter most. This is not coincidental — the CETT metric captures neurons whose outputs correlate with the model's final answer token probabilities, and those neurons cluster in the layers that carry the strongest causal signal.

The scaling from 0.036‰ (500 samples) to 0.319‰ (4183 samples) is expected: L1 regression finds sparser solutions with fewer examples. More data reveals more neurons that genuinely predict correctness beyond noise.

## 9) Movie OOD — True Zero-Knowledge Baseline

**Question:**
Does a model trained on medical data show the expected 3-way accuracy gradient when tested on post-training-cutoff movie data

**Bottom line:**
**Yes, the accuracy gradient holds: movie_ood (50.1%, chance) < medbullets (55.2%) < afrimedqa (70.1%).** However, **L61 norms for movie_ood are unexpectedly high** — higher than medbullets despite lower accuracy. The model answers movie questions with high confidence but incorrectly (confabulation), producing strong decision-layer representations for wrong answers. This reveals that L61 norm captures answer commitment, not factual accuracy.

**Setup:**
- Dataset: `movie_ood` — 1,572 MCQ questions from TMDB post-Aug-2025 movies, 4 question types (director, cast, genre, country)
- Built via: `scripts/build_movie_ood_dataset.py` with TMDB API
- Experiment: `residual_norm_ood`, chain_of_thought prompt
- Run: `outputs/2026-03-13/08-31-59`

**Key findings:**

| Dataset | Accuracy | AUROC L2 Norm | AUROC Entropy | L61 Mean Norm (correct) |
|---------|----------|---------------|---------------|------------------------|
| movie_ood | 0.501 | 0.614 | 0.692 | ~16,615 (unexpectedly high) |
| medbullets | 0.552 | 0.606 | 0.648 | — |
| afrimedqa | 0.701 | 0.557–0.627 | 0.696–0.735 | — |

**Accuracy gradient confirms OOD hypothesis:**
- movie_ood: random chance (50.1%) — as expected
- medbullets: OOD medical (55.2%) — as expected
- afrimedqa: non-OOD medical (70.1%) — as expected

**Unexpected norm finding:**
movie_ood L61 norms are higher than expected from the OOD hypothesis. The model produces strong internal representations for movie questions — but these are wrong answers expressed confidently. This is the **confabulation signature**: MedGemma generates plausible-sounding film knowledge that doesn't exist, with high-norm "decision signals" pointing to wrong answers. The correct/incorrect split for movie_ood is essentially random (50/50), confirming zero factual knowledge of post-cutoff movies.

**Interpretation:**
The movie_ood experiment succeeds as a methodological proof of concept: accuracy at chance confirms the dataset is genuinely outside the training distribution. The confabulation finding is a useful secondary result. It shows that L61 norm cannot distinguish "genuine knowledge → right answer" from "confident confabulation → wrong answer." Entropy is more discriminative because it reflects the model's uncertainty over letter choices, not just the magnitude of its internal state.

**Caveats:**
- Question types (director, cast, genre, country) may differ in difficulty; genre questions may be partially guessable without factual knowledge

## 10) Answers to Sprint Questions

### Q1 — Which token types does L3 causally gate on?

**Answer: Choice tokens dominate, but higher choice/content attention at L3 predicts wrong answers.**
- Choice tokens are the dominant group at L3 (importance: 0.21–0.28 across datasets)
- Negative correlations: r=−0.105 to −0.186 (choice), r=−0.114 to −0.175 (content), p<0.01 across all valid datasets
- Delimiter tokens: neutral to mildly positive (r=+0.01 to +0.11)
- L3 is a **surface-matching layer**: it locks onto answer choice labels and clinical content. When the model's attention over-indexes on these surface features, it is more likely to be wrong.

### Q2 — Do SAEs reveal histopathology-specific features that amplify with few-shot?

**Answer: Phase 1 confirmed (L26 #12445 identified). Phase 2 negative — direction reversed, pattern is domain-type specific not histo-specific.**
- L26 feature #12445 is the most discriminative histo feature (score 13,528) — stable across all 12 datasets
- Unexpectedly, L60 (#14443, score 114,742) is a far stronger histo-discriminative layer than L26
- Specialty datasets (histo, cardiology, neurology, oncology): histo features suppressed at L26 (mean effect −0.95 to −6.19)
- General MCQ datasets (afrimedqa, medbullets, medqa, plab): histo features mildly amplified (+0.15 to +0.97)
- The performance boost from few-shot on histopathology (+0.675 in 27.02) is not explained by L26 feature amplification.
- **Caveat**: (cardiology/neurology/oncology suppression may be artefact of parse failures; larger-sample rerun needed)

### Q3 — Can L61 norm serve as a per-sample OOD flag?

**Answer: No — entropy is better. L61 norm fails on 5/7 datasets (AUROC < 0.6); entropy passes on 5/7.**
- L61 norm AUROC: afrimedqa 0.627, medbullets 0.533–0.606, medmcqa 0.594, medqa 0.581, mmlu_medical 0.655, plab 0.590 — passes >0.6 on 2/6
- Entropy AUROC: afrimedqa 0.696, medbullets 0.648–0.692, medmcqa 0.728, medqa 0.732, mmlu_medical 0.847, plab 0.712 — passes >0.6 on 5/6

### Q4 — Can composite L61 + L3 entropy detect distribution shift?

**Answer: No — Mahalanobis AUROC is at chance (0.484–0.560) across all valid datasets.**
- The composite adds no discriminative power over entropy alone
- The two signals (norm = confidence, entropy = uncertainty) do not combine into a useful shift detector
